Is climate change eating away at Tromsø's skiing season?

Sometimes I like to ski :)

Not downhill skiing. You might die and break your neck. Or the other way around. Either way, it's not for me.

I like crosscountry skiing. You know, the type of skiing where you walk leisurely over the snow? Small uphills, small downhills. With snack breaks.

However, when I moved to Tromsø, a small town of 80,000 people in the Arctic part of Norway, I was sad to find that the winter temperatures fluctuated wildly.

One week there'd be nearly half a metre of snow on the ground and nice, dry temperatures of -5 °C. The next week, temperatures would rise to just above freezing, making the ground slushy and soggy. And it would rain.

Somebody told me: "This is the new normal."

Then I found this piece of terrifying journalism from NRK, the public broadcaster in Norway.

"Today, Tromsø typically has around 113 ski days in the lowlands in an average year. This means days with at least 20 centimetres of snow on the ground," it reads, while a happy animated person skis in the background.

Then: "By the end of the century, researchers estimate that nearly two-thirds of those ski days will be gone."

The happy man is no longer happy. They've stopped skiing. Now they're just standing there. It's raining.

According to NRK, the number of ski days in a season will decrease from 113 today to 40 before 2100. And they say the changes have already begun.

That's what this tiny project is for.

Let's check if climate change is already eating away at the skiing season in Tromsø.

Warmer temperatures! Less snow?

Thanks to a great API from The Norwegian Meteorological Institute, I've been able to get data on the snow depth in Tromsø for every hour since 1920. The same goes for air temperature.

After hours of analysing the data, there is no doubt about the fact that the air temperature during the skiing seasons in Tromsø has increased.

But what about the amount of snow? Are there fewer days with enough snow cover to ski than there used to be?

To figure that out, I had to count the number of days in every skiing season with a snow cover deeper than 30 centimetres.

(Yes, I know NRK used 20 centimetres, but I don't want my precious skis to accidentally hit a rock, so we're going with 30 centimetres for good measure.)

Wow, what happened in the twenties??

In a six year period, there were four years with less than 50 ski days in a season. Outrageous.

If we compare the amount of ski days in the last ten years with that, it could look like we have nothing to worry about.

But there's no doubt that there were consistently more seasons with a lot of ski days in the last half of the 20th century than there has been the last 24 years.

Time will tell if NRK is right when they say that nearly two-thirds of our ski days will be disappear before year 2100.

Some notes about data and stuff

People in Tromsø call March and April the biggest snow months. But if you're lucky, you can already ski in October - and sometimes even in May. That's why I defined the skiing season to be October through May.

The charts were made with Flourish, using data from the Frost API that I've collected, cleaned and analysed.

There is plenty more that I would have liked to do for this project - more analyses, better charts, there's so much that could have been done - but time is scarce...
